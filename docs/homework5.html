HMWK 5 – Measures of Location & Dispersion

Research on different types of location and dispersion measures (mean, median, MSE, variance, standard deviation, IQR, MAD, ...) and their typical uses, advantages and limitations.

1) Concepts & Taxonomy

Measures of location (or central tendency) describe a typical value of a distribution, while measures of dispersion quantify how spread out the data are around that typical value.

FamilyMeasureDefinition (informal)Notes
Location Arithmetic mean Average of values: $\displaystyle \bar x = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ Minimizes mean squared error; very sensitive to outliers and skew.
Median Middle value (50th percentile) Robust to outliers; defined for ordinal data; can be less efficient than mean under normality.
Mode Most frequent value or local maximum of density Can reveal multimodality; depends on binning for continuous data.
Trimmed mean Mean after removing lowest and highest $p\%$ of values Compromise between mean and median; reduces influence of extremes.
Geometric mean $\big(\prod x_i\big)^{1/n}$ for $x_i>0$ Used for growth rates, ratios, log-normal data; undefined with zeros or negatives.
Harmonic mean $\displaystyle n\Big/\sum \frac1{x_i}$ Appropriate for rates (e.g. speed); dominated by small values.
Dispersion Mean squared error (MSE) $\displaystyle \text{MSE}(\hat\theta)=\mathbb{E}\big[(\hat\theta-\theta)^2\big]$ Combines variance and bias; optimization target in many estimators.
Variance Mean squared deviation: $\displaystyle \sigma^2=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]$ Key theoretical quantity; in squared units.
Sample variance $\displaystyle s^2 = \frac1{n-1}\sum (x_i-\bar x)^2$ Unbiased for population variance when sampling IID.
Standard deviation $\sqrt{\text{variance}}$ Same units as data; widely reported; sensitive to outliers.
Interquartile range (IQR) $Q_3-Q_1$ Robust spread around the median; ignores extreme tails.
Median absolute deviation (MAD) $\operatorname{median}\big(|x_i-\operatorname{median}(x)|\big)$ Very robust to outliers; often scaled (e.g. $1.4826\times\text{MAD}$) to match SD under normality.

Location vs Dispersion: Always report at least one of each (e.g. mean & SD or median & IQR) to give both “where” and “how spread”.

2) Uses, Advantages, Limitations

MeasureBest suited forAdvantagesLimitations
Mean Symmetric, light‑tailed distributions; additive noise models. Algebra-friendly; appears in CLT and many optimality results; easy to compute. Non-robust to outliers; distorted by skewed data.
Median Skewed or heavy‑tailed data; ordinal scales. Robust to outliers; minimizes sum of absolute deviations. Less efficient than mean when data are truly normal; harder to manipulate algebraically.
Mode Discrete or categorical data; detecting multiple peaks. Highlights “most common” category or value. Can depend strongly on binning for continuous data; may be non-unique.
Variance / SD Measurement error analysis, regression, ANOVA, forecasting. Supports variance decompositions; central in Gaussian models. Not robust to outliers; variance in squared units can be hard to interpret directly.
IQR Exploratory data analysis; boxplots; robust summaries. Insensitive to extreme tails; easy to visualize. Ignores information in the tails; less efficient under normality.
MAD Robust dispersion with strong outliers or non-Gaussian noise. Very robust; often used in robust regression and outlier detection. Needs scaling to be comparable to SD under normality.

3) Summary Guidelines

  • For approximately symmetric, light‑tailed data: report mean ± standard deviation.
  • For clearly skewed or heavy‑tailed data: report median and a robust spread like IQR or MAD.
  • For rates or multiplicative growth: use geometric or harmonic means as appropriate.
  • In research or modeling, MSE is often minimized when choosing or evaluating estimators.
